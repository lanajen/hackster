DIY Relay Outlet Arduino – Maker Guide Episode 7

Posted on November 15, 2013 by benjones

This video shows you how to build a solid state relay outlet to control a variety of high power devices. Please watch Episode 6 to learn how to build the relay outlet.

If you are not comfortable working with mains electricity do not attempt this build. I take no responsibility for any damage done to you or your home.

Parts List:

Solid State Relay – http://www.ebay.com/itm/Solid-State-Relay-SSR-25DA-25A-250V-3-32VDC-/290631646547

Outlet – Lowes/Home Depot
Outlet Box – Lowes/Home Depot
Outlet Box Cover – Lowes/Home Depot
Romex Connector – Lowes/Home Depot
an Old Computer Power Cord – or 12 gauge replacement appliance cord for high power applications
Two #8-32 half inch machine screws, nuts, and washers – Lowes/Home Depot

Arduino Uno – http://www.amazon.com/Arduino-UNO-board-DIP-ATmega328P/dp/B006H06TVG/

Some 22 gauge wire

  7. Rich Russell on November 27, 2013 at 9:55 am said:

    Ty – There is no relay or coil involved, an SSR is effectively an opto-isolated triac. The low voltage (trigger) current is only 7.5mA at 12V (maybe around 16mA at 5V) according to the datasheet. It is after all not much more than an LED and resistor. That’s well within the capabilities of an Arduino output, and as it’s not inductive there’s no back current when it switches off so no diode required.
    The zero crossing detector and triac circuit on the high voltage side may take a 200mA or more, but it’s taken from the supply to the load not the Arduino. SSRs do get warm when switched on, so there obviously is some consumption of current.
